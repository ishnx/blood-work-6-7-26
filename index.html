Ch. 12The Pareto Prescription

Four interventions. One of them does most of the work.

Ranked by the biological leverage each move buys you — the ratio of effort invested to root causes resolved.

Highest Leverage
01

Hydrate deliberately, and spread your protein across the day

Effort: trivial  ·  Cost: ₹0  ·  Resolves: 4 of 5 flagged findings

Drink 3.5–4 litres of water daily, front-load a glass with every meal, and instead of one or two large protein-heavy meals (or a big post-workout shake), spread your protein intake into 3–4 smaller doses through the day, each paired with water and fibrous vegetables.

Why this works biologically: Your elevated urea, BUN, BUN/creatinine ratio, and phosphorus are the classic signature of a body metabolising more protein than it's diluting — a "pre-renal" pattern, not kidney damage (your eGFR of 111 proves the filter itself is excellent). Urea is a nitrogen-waste by-product of protein breakdown; it's cleared by the kidneys but concentrates in the blood when water intake can't keep pace with protein load. Phosphorus rises through the same dietary channel — meat, dairy, and supplements are phosphorus-dense. Adding water increases glomerular flow and dilutes concentration; spreading protein intake reduces the peak nitrogen load your liver has to convert to urea at any one time.
02

Swap saturated fat for fibre and unsaturated fat — don't fear the whole panel

Effort: moderate  ·  Cost: ₹0–low  ·  Resolves: total cholesterol & LDL trend

Add 10–15g of soluble fibre daily (oats, psyllium husk, legumes, apples), replace ghee/butter/red meat fat with olive oil, nuts, and fatty fish 2–3x/week, and keep added sugar and refined carbs low so triglycerides — already excellent at 75.8 — stay that way.

Why this works biologically: Soluble fibre binds bile acids in the gut, forcing the liver to pull cholesterol out of circulation to manufacture replacement bile acids — a direct, mechanical drain on circulating LDL. Swapping saturated fat reduces the raw material your liver uses to package new LDL particles (apoB-lipoproteins). Because your HDL, triglycerides, and Apo B/A1 ratio are already excellent, this is a "turn one dial" fix, not a metabolic overhaul.
03

Get one confirmatory blood draw: HPLC / haemoglobin electrophoresis + iron panel

Effort: one appointment  ·  Cost: low  ·  Resolves: the blood cell mystery, permanently

Ask your physician for a haemoglobin HPLC (or electrophoresis) plus a full iron panel (TIBC, transferrin saturation) — a single blood draw that will tell you definitively whether the RBC/MCV/MCH pattern in Chapter 4 is a thalassemia trait or something else.

Why this works biologically: Right now, three independent indices (Mentzer, RDWI, Green & King) all lean toward an inherited trait rather than iron deficiency — but a trait diagnosis changes what you should not do (don't self-supplement iron; excess iron has nowhere useful to go and can accumulate) as much as what you should. It also carries a practical, high-value implication for the future: if you or a partner both carry the trait, that's meaningful information before starting a family.
04

Re-test hsCRP in 4 months, after tightening sleep & processed food intake

Effort: low, passive  ·  Cost: ₹0  ·  Resolves: confirms whether the inflammation blip was noise

hsCRP at 1.28 mg/L is barely over the "optimal" line of 1.0 — not remotely alarming, but worth re-checking once (as your lab already recommends) after normal sleep, hydration, and a lower load of fried/processed food.

Why this works biologically: hsCRP is produced by the liver within hours of almost any inflammatory signal — a late night, a minor gum infection, even a hard workout can nudge it. A single reading this close to threshold is more likely biological noise than a true signal; a repeat test resolves the ambiguity cheaply.
